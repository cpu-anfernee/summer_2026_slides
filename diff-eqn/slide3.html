Chapter 2.2

Separable Equations

Solving First-Order Differential Equations by Separation of Variables

What is a Separable Equation?

A first-order differential equation is separable if it can be written in the form:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)p(y) \]

Or, equivalently, by letting \( h(y) = \frac{1}{p(y)} \):

\[ h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x) \]

The key characteristic is that the expression can be factored into a function of entirely \( x \) multiplied by a function of entirely \( y \).

Method of Solution

  1. Separate the variables: Multiply/divide to get all \( y \)'s (including \( dy \)) on one side and all \( x \)'s (including \( dx \)) on the other.
    \[ h(y) dy = g(x) dx \]
  2. Integrate both sides:
    \[ \int h(y) dy = \int g(x) dx \]
  3. Combine constants: Both integrals produce a constant of integration, but they can be combined into a single arbitrary constant \( C \) on the right side.
  4. Solve for y: If possible, solve the resulting equation explicitly for \( y \). Otherwise, leave it as an implicit solution.

Example 1: Basic Algebraic

Solve: \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x}{y} \)

  1. Separate: Multiply both sides by \( y \) and \( dx \).
    \( y \, dy = x \, dx \)
  2. Integrate:
    \( \int y \, dy = \int x \, dx \)
    \( \frac{y^2}{2} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C_1 \)
  3. Simplify: Multiply by 2 to clear fractions.
    \( y^2 = x^2 + 2C_1 \)
    Let \( C = 2C_1 \).
  4. Final Implicit Solution:
    \( y^2 - x^2 = C \)
    (This represents a family of hyperbolas).

Example 2: Exponential Functions

Solve: \( \frac{dy}{dx} = x e^{-y} \)

  1. Separate: Multiply by \( e^y \) and \( dx \).
    \( e^y \, dy = x \, dx \)
  2. Integrate:
    \( \int e^y \, dy = \int x \, dx \)
    \( e^y = \frac{x^2}{2} + C \)
  3. Solve Explicitly: Take the natural logarithm of both sides.
    \( \ln(e^y) = \ln\left(\frac{x^2}{2} + C\right) \)
  4. Final Explicit Solution:
    \( y(x) = \ln\left(\frac{x^2}{2} + C\right) \)

Example 3: Rational Functions

Solve: \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x^2 - 1}{y^2 + 1} \)

  1. Separate: Cross-multiply to group variables.
    \( (y^2 + 1) \, dy = (x^2 - 1) \, dx \)
  2. Integrate:
    \( \int (y^2 + 1) \, dy = \int (x^2 - 1) \, dx \)
    \( \frac{y^3}{3} + y = \frac{x^3}{3} - x + C_1 \)
  3. Simplify: Multiply by 3 for a cleaner look (optional).
    \( y^3 + 3y = x^3 - 3x + 3C_1 \)
  4. Final Implicit Solution:
    \( y^3 + 3y = x^3 - 3x + C \)
    (Because solving for \( y \) explicitly requires the cubic formula, leaving it implicit is standard practice here).

Example 4: Initial Value Problem (IVP)

Solve: \( \frac{dy}{dx} = xy^2 \) , given \( y(0) = 1 \)

  1. Separate: Divide by \( y^2 \) (assuming \( y \neq 0 \)).
    \( y^{-2} \, dy = x \, dx \)
  2. Integrate:
    \( \frac{y^{-1}}{-1} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C \implies -\frac{1}{y} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C \)
  3. Apply Initial Condition: Plug in \( x=0, y=1 \).
    \( -\frac{1}{1} = \frac{0^2}{2} + C \implies C = -1 \)
  4. Substitute C back:
    \( -\frac{1}{y} = \frac{x^2}{2} - 1 \implies -\frac{1}{y} = \frac{x^2 - 2}{2} \)
  5. Final Explicit Solution: Take the reciprocal.
    \( -y = \frac{2}{x^2 - 2} \implies y(x) = \frac{2}{2 - x^2} \)

Example 5: Careful Factoring

Solve: \( \frac{dy}{dx} = xy + 2x - y - 2 \)

At first glance, this doesn't look separable. We must factor by grouping first!

  1. Factor the RHS:
    \( xy + 2x - (y + 2) \)
    \( x(y + 2) - 1(y + 2) \)
    \( \frac{dy}{dx} = (x - 1)(y + 2) \)
  2. Separate:
    \( \frac{1}{y + 2} \, dy = (x - 1) \, dx \)
  3. Integrate:
    \( \ln|y + 2| = \frac{x^2}{2} - x + C \)
  4. Solve Explicitly: Exponentiate both sides.
    \( |y + 2| = e^{x^2/2 - x + C} = e^C \cdot e^{x^2/2 - x} \)
    Let \( K = \pm e^C \) (an arbitrary constant).
    Final Answer: \( y(x) = K e^{x^2/2 - x} - 2 \)
