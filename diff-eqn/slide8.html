Chapters 3.2 & 3.3

Growth, Decay & Cooling

Applications of First-Order Differential Equations

Part 1: Exponential Growth & Decay

In many natural phenomena, the rate of change of a quantity $P$ is proportional to the amount present at that time.

\[ \frac{dP}{dt} = kP \implies P(t) = P_0 e^{kt} \]
  • $P_0$: The initial amount at time $t = 0$.
  • $k > 0$: Exponential Growth (populations, investments).
  • $k < 0$: Exponential Decay (radioactive isotopes, drug elimination).

Example 1: Population Forecasting

A city's population was $10,000$ in the year 2000. In 2010, it was $15,000$. Assuming exponential growth, what will it be in 2030?

  1. Set up the Model: Let $t=0$ be the year 2000. Therefore, $P_0 = 10000$.
    $P(t) = 10000 e^{kt}$
  2. Find the Growth Rate ($k$): Use the data for 2010 ($t=10$).
    $15000 = 10000 e^{10k} \implies 1.5 = e^{10k}$
  3. Solve for $k$:
    $\ln(1.5) = 10k \implies k = \frac{\ln 1.5}{10} \approx 0.0405$
  4. Predict for 2030 ($t=30$):
    $P(30) = 10000 e^{30k}$
  5. Evaluate (Algebra Shortcut!): Since $e^{10k} = 1.5$, then $e^{30k} = (e^{10k})^3 = (1.5)^3$.
    $P(30) = 10000(1.5)^3 = 10000(3.375)$
  6. Final Answer: $33,750$ people.

Example 2: Bacterial Doubling Time

A bacteria culture doubles every 3 hours. If there are $100$ bacteria initially, how many will there be after 15 hours?

  1. Use the Doubling Time Formula: $T = \frac{\ln 2}{k}$.
    $3 = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \frac{\ln 2}{3}$
  2. Write the Specific Equation:
    $P(t) = 100 e^{(\frac{\ln 2}{3})t}$
  3. Evaluate at $t = 15$:
    $P(15) = 100 e^{(\frac{\ln 2}{3})15} = 100 e^{5 \ln 2}$
  4. Simplify using Log Rules: $e^{5 \ln 2} = e^{\ln(2^5)} = 2^5 = 32$.
  5. Final Answer:
    $P(15) = 100(32) = 3,200$ bacteria.

Example 3: Radioactive Half-Life

Carbon-14 has a half-life of 5,730 years. Find the exact decay constant $k$.

  1. Set up the Condition: At $t = 5730$, the amount remaining is $0.5 P_0$.
    $0.5 P_0 = P_0 e^{5730k}$
  2. Divide by $P_0$:
    $0.5 = e^{5730k}$
  3. Take the Natural Log:
    $\ln(0.5) = 5730k$
  4. Solve for $k$:
    $k = \frac{\ln(0.5)}{5730}$
  5. Final Answer: Since $\ln(0.5) = -\ln 2$, we write:
    $k = \frac{-\ln 2}{5730} \approx -0.000121$

Example 4: Carbon Dating

A fossil bone contains 20% of its original Carbon-14. How old is the fossil? (Use $k = \frac{-\ln 2}{5730}$).

  1. Set up the Equation: We are looking for $t$ when $P(t) = 0.20 P_0$.
    $0.20 P_0 = P_0 e^{kt}$
  2. Isolate the Exponential:
    $0.20 = e^{kt}$
  3. Take the Natural Log:
    $\ln(0.20) = kt$
  4. Substitute $k$ and Solve for $t$:
    $t = \frac{\ln(0.20)}{k} = \frac{\ln(0.20)}{-\ln 2 / 5730}$
  5. Final Answer (approximate):
    $t = -5730 \frac{\ln(0.2)}{\ln 2} \approx 13,304$ years old.

Example 5: Drug Elimination

A drug is eliminated from the bloodstream exponentially. $50$ mg are injected. After 2 hours, $30$ mg remain. Find the half-life.

  1. Find the Decay Constant ($k$): $P_0 = 50$, $P(2) = 30$.
    $30 = 50 e^{2k} \implies 0.6 = e^{2k}$
  2. Solve for $k$:
    $\ln(0.6) = 2k \implies k = \frac{\ln 0.6}{2}$
  3. Use the Half-Life Formula:
    $T = \frac{-\ln 2}{k}$
  4. Substitute $k$:
    $T = \frac{-\ln 2}{(\ln 0.6)/2} = \frac{-2 \ln 2}{\ln 0.6}$
  5. Final Answer:
    $T \approx \frac{-1.386}{-0.5108} \approx 2.71$ hours.

Example 6: Targeting a Specific Multiplier

A population grows at a continuous rate of 5% per year ($k = 0.05$). How long will it take for the population to triple?

  1. Set up the Equation: We want $P(t) = 3P_0$.
    $3P_0 = P_0 e^{0.05t}$
  2. Divide by $P_0$:
    $3 = e^{0.05t}$
  3. Take the Natural Log:
    $\ln(3) = 0.05t$
  4. Solve for $t$:
    $t = \frac{\ln 3}{0.05} = 20 \ln 3$
  5. Final Answer:
    $t \approx 21.97$ years.

Part 2: Newton's Law of Cooling

The rate at which an object cools (or warms) is proportional to the difference between its temperature $T$ and the ambient temperature $T_m$ of its surrounding medium.

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_m) \]

The Solution

By separating variables ($\frac{dT}{T - T_m} = k dt$) and integrating, we get:

\[ T(t) = T_m + (T_0 - T_m)e^{kt} \]

Where $T_0$ is the initial temperature of the object. Note that $k < 0$ for both heating and cooling in this standard form.

Example 7: Basic Cooling

A cup of coffee cools from $90^\circ$C to $70^\circ$C in 10 minutes in a $20^\circ$C room. Find its temperature at 20 minutes.

  1. Identify Constants: $T_m = 20$, $T_0 = 90$.
    $T(t) = 20 + (90 - 20)e^{kt} = 20 + 70e^{kt}$
  2. Find the Cooling Constant ($k$): Use $T(10) = 70$.
    $70 = 20 + 70e^{10k} \implies 50 = 70e^{10k} \implies e^{10k} = \frac{5}{7}$
  3. Set up for $t=20$:
    $T(20) = 20 + 70e^{20k}$
  4. Evaluate (Algebra Shortcut): Notice that $e^{20k} = (e^{10k})^2$.
    $e^{20k} = \left(\frac{5}{7}\right)^2 = \frac{25}{49}$
  5. Calculate:
    $T(20) = 20 + 70\left(\frac{25}{49}\right) = 20 + 10\left(\frac{25}{7}\right) = 20 + \frac{250}{7}$
  6. Final Answer: $20 + 35.71 = 55.71^\circ$C.

Example 8: Time of Death (Forensics)

A body is found at $85^\circ$F in a $70^\circ$F room. 1 hour later, it is $80^\circ$F. Assuming a normal living temp of $98.6^\circ$F, how long before discovery did they die?

  1. Set up the Model: Let $t=0$ be the time of discovery. $T_m = 70$, $T_0 = 85$.
    $T(t) = 70 + 15e^{kt}$
  2. Find $k$: At $t = 1$ hour, $T = 80$.
    $80 = 70 + 15e^{k} \implies 10 = 15e^k \implies e^k = \frac{2}{3} \implies k = \ln(2/3) \approx -0.4055$
  3. Find Time of Death ($t$): We want $t$ (which will be negative) when $T = 98.6$.
    $98.6 = 70 + 15e^{kt} \implies 28.6 = 15e^{kt}$
  4. Solve for $t$:
    $e^{kt} = \frac{28.6}{15} \approx 1.9067 \implies kt = \ln(1.9067)$
  5. Calculate: $t = \frac{\ln(1.9067)}{\ln(2/3)} \approx \frac{0.6454}{-0.4055} \approx -1.59$
  6. Final Answer: The person died approximately $1.59$ hours (about 1 hr 35 mins) before the body was discovered.

Example 9: Newton's Law of Warming

A pizza at $20^\circ$C is placed in a $200^\circ$C oven. After 5 minutes, it is $100^\circ$C. When will it reach $150^\circ$C?

The exact same formula applies, the coefficients will just be negative!

  1. Identify Constants: $T_m = 200$, $T_0 = 20$.
    $T(t) = 200 + (20 - 200)e^{kt} = 200 - 180e^{kt}$
  2. Find $k$: At $t = 5$, $T = 100$.
    $100 = 200 - 180e^{5k} \implies -100 = -180e^{5k} \implies e^{5k} = \frac{5}{9}$
  3. Set up for $T = 150$:
    $150 = 200 - 180e^{kt} \implies -50 = -180e^{kt} \implies e^{kt} = \frac{5}{18}$
  4. Solve for $t$:
    $kt = \ln(5/18) \implies t = \frac{\ln(5/18)}{k}$. Since $k = \frac{\ln(5/9)}{5}$, we have $t = \frac{5 \ln(5/18)}{\ln(5/9)}$.
  5. Final Answer:
    $t \approx \frac{5(-1.2809)}{-0.5878} \approx 10.89$ minutes.

Example 10: Extreme Cooling

A $1000^\circ$C metal bar is placed in a $0^\circ$C bath. It cools to $500^\circ$C in 2 minutes. When will it be $100^\circ$C?

  1. Identify Constants: $T_m = 0$, $T_0 = 1000$.
    $T(t) = 0 + (1000 - 0)e^{kt} = 1000e^{kt}$
  2. Notice the Half-Life: It went from $1000^\circ$ to $500^\circ$ (half the temp) in 2 minutes. The half-life is 2 mins!
    $k = \frac{-\ln 2}{2}$
  3. Set up for $T = 100$:
    $100 = 1000e^{kt} \implies e^{kt} = 0.1$
  4. Solve for $t$:
    $kt = \ln(0.1) \implies t = \frac{\ln(0.1)}{k}$
  5. Substitute $k$:
    $t = \frac{\ln(10^{-1})}{-\ln 2 / 2} = \frac{-\ln 10}{-\ln 2 / 2} = 2 \frac{\ln 10}{\ln 2}$
  6. Final Answer:
    $t \approx 2(3.32) = 6.64$ minutes.
