Chapters 5.4 & 5.5

Integration of Exponential Functions

Base \( e \) and Bases Other Than \( e \)

Part 1: The Natural Exponential Function (Base \( e \))

The natural exponential function is unique because it is its own derivative. Consequently, it is its own antiderivative.

Basic Rule:

\[ \int e^x dx = e^x + C \]

General Rule (with substitution): If \( u \) is a differentiable function of \( x \):

\[ \int e^u du = e^u + C \]

(Press Down Arrow for 5 Examples)

Example 1: Linear Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int e^{3x} dx \)

  1. Set up \( u \): Let \( u \) be the exponent.
    \( u = 3x \)
  2. Find \( du \):
    \( du = 3 dx \implies dx = \frac{du}{3} \)
  3. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int e^u \left(\frac{du}{3}\right) = \frac{1}{3} \int e^u du = \frac{1}{3}e^u + C \)
  4. Final Answer:
    \( \frac{1}{3}e^{3x} + C \)

Example 2: Polynomial Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int 5x e^{-x^2} dx \)

  1. Set up \( u \): Let the exponent be \( u \).
    \( u = -x^2 \)
  2. Find \( du \) and adjust:
    \( du = -2x dx \). We have \( 5x dx \).
    Multiply by \( -\frac{5}{2} \) to match: \( -\frac{5}{2} du = 5x dx \)
  3. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int e^u \left(-\frac{5}{2} du\right) = -\frac{5}{2} \int e^u du = -\frac{5}{2} e^u + C \)
  4. Final Answer:
    \( -\frac{5}{2}e^{-x^2} + C \)

Example 3: Rational Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int \frac{e^{1/x}}{x^2} dx \)

  1. Rewrite for clarity:
    \( \int e^{x^{-1}} \cdot x^{-2} dx \)
  2. Set up \( u \): Let \( u = x^{-1} \).
  3. Find \( du \):
    \( du = -x^{-2} dx \implies -du = x^{-2} dx = \frac{1}{x^2} dx \)
  4. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int e^u (-du) = -e^u + C \)
  5. Final Answer:
    \( -e^{1/x} + C \)

Example 4: Trigonometric Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int \cos(x) e^{\sin x} dx \)

  1. Identify Pattern: The derivative of the exponent is multiplying the exponential function.
  2. Set up \( u \): Let \( u = \sin x \).
  3. Find \( du \):
    \( du = \cos x dx \)
  4. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int e^u du = e^u + C \)
  5. Final Answer:
    \( e^{\sin x} + C \)

Example 5: Algebraic Expansion

Evaluate: \( \int (e^x + e^{-x})^2 dx \)

Wait! Substitution won't work immediately. We must expand the binomial first.

  1. Expand the squared term:
    \( (e^x + e^{-x})(e^x + e^{-x}) = (e^x)^2 + 2(e^x)(e^{-x}) + (e^{-x})^2 \)
  2. Simplify exponents:
    \( e^{2x} + 2(e^0) + e^{-2x} = e^{2x} + 2 + e^{-2x} \)
  3. Integrate term by term:
    \( \int e^{2x} dx + \int 2 dx + \int e^{-2x} dx \)
  4. Apply linear substitution for each:
    \( \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} + 2x + \frac{1}{-2}e^{-2x} + C \)
  5. Final Answer:
    \( \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} + 2x - \frac{1}{2}e^{-2x} + C \)

Part 2: Bases Other Than \( e \)

When integrating an exponential function with a base \( a \) (where \( a > 0, a \neq 1 \)), the process is almost identical to base \( e \), but we must divide by the natural log of the base.

Basic Rule:

\[ \int a^x dx = \frac{a^x}{\ln a} + C \]

General Rule (with substitution): If \( u \) is a differentiable function of \( x \):

\[ \int a^u du = \frac{a^u}{\ln a} + C \]

(Press Down Arrow for 5 Examples)

Example 6: Basic Integration

Evaluate: \( \int 3^x dx \)

  1. Identify the Base:
    Here, \( a = 3 \).
  2. Apply the Rule Directly:
    Since the exponent is just \( x \), no substitution is needed.
  3. Final Answer:
    \( \frac{3^x}{\ln 3} + C \)

Example 7: Linear Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int 5^{-x} dx \)

  1. Set up \( u \): Let \( u = -x \).
  2. Find \( du \):
    \( du = -dx \implies -du = dx \)
  3. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int 5^u (-du) = -\int 5^u du = -\left(\frac{5^u}{\ln 5}\right) + C \)
  4. Final Answer:
    \( -\frac{5^{-x}}{\ln 5} + C \)

Example 8: Polynomial Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int x 2^{x^2} dx \)

  1. Set up \( u \): Let \( u = x^2 \).
  2. Find \( du \):
    \( du = 2x dx \). We have \( x dx \), so divide by 2: \( \frac{du}{2} = x dx \).
  3. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int 2^u \left(\frac{du}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{2} \int 2^u du = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{2^u}{\ln 2} \right) + C \)
  4. Final Answer:
    \( \frac{2^{x^2}}{2 \ln 2} + C \)

Example 9: Trigonometric Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int \sin(x) 6^{\cos x} dx \)

  1. Set up \( u \): Let \( u = \cos x \).
  2. Find \( du \):
    \( du = -\sin x dx \implies -du = \sin x dx \).
  3. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int 6^u (-du) = -\int 6^u du = -\left( \frac{6^u}{\ln 6} \right) + C \)
  4. Final Answer:
    \( -\frac{6^{\cos x}}{\ln 6} + C \)

Example 10: Radical Exponent

Evaluate: \( \int \frac{7^{\sqrt{x}}}{\sqrt{x}} dx \)

  1. Set up \( u \): Let \( u = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \).
  2. Find \( du \):
    \( du = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} dx = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} dx \).
  3. Adjust constants:
    We have \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} dx \). Multiply by 2: \( 2 du = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} dx \).
  4. Substitute and Integrate:
    \( \int 7^u (2 du) = 2 \int 7^u du = 2 \left( \frac{7^u}{\ln 7} \right) + C \)
  5. Final Answer:
    \( \frac{2 \cdot 7^{\sqrt{x}}}{\ln 7} + C \)
